Inequalities and Their Graphs

An inequality compares two values that are not always equal.

Inequality Symbols

Symbol Meaning
< Less than
> Greater than
≤ Less than or equal to
≥ Greater than or equal to
Example:
x < 5 means x is less than 5.

x ≥ 3 means x is greater than or equal to 3.

Identifying Solutions by Evaluating

To check if a number is a solution, plug the number into the inequality.

Example:
x + 4 > 9

Check if x = 6 is a solution:
6 + 4 > 9
10 > 9

This is true, so 6 is a solution.
Example:

Inequality: 1/2(x) + 6 > 12
Ordered Pair: (6, 12)

Check if x = 6 is a solution:
1/2(6) + 6 > 12
9 > 12

This is false, so 6 is not a solution.

Graphing Linear Inequalities

Note:
  • The shaded area of a graph represents values greater than the function.
  • The non-shaded are of a graaph represents values less than the function.
Example of a linear inequality: y ≤ -2x + 5

Dotted Line

Use a dotted line when the inequality is either < or >

The dotted line means the values on the line are not included.

Example: y > 3x

To put it into perspective:
- If we substitute -1 for x, y = -3 however, -3 is not included in the shaded area of the graph.

Note: the dotted or shaded line represents the function of the graph

Solid Line

Use a solid line when the inequality is either ≤ or ≥

The solid line means the values on the line are included.

Example: y ≤ 2x + 3

To put it into perspective:
- If we substitute -1 for x and get y = 1, that "1" would be included in the shaded area of the graph.

Note: the dotted or shaded line represents the function of the graph

Shading

After drawing the line, shade the correct side.

  • If the inequality is y > or ≥ mx + b, shade above the line.
  • If the inequality is y < or ≤ mx + b, shade below the line.

Writing an Inequality from a Graph

How to write an inequality from a graph (order doesn't entirely matter):

  1. Check if the graph is shaded above or below the line

  2. Check if the line is dotted or solid and determine whether the inequality is <, >, ≤, or ≥

  3. Find the slope and y-intercept of the line

  4. Write the inequality out substituting the = sign for one of the four inequality signs
y < 5x - 1
Note: the dotted or shaded line represents the function of the graph

Solving Inequalities Using Addition and Subtraction

Solving inequalities using addition and subtraction follows the same process as solving equations.

The goal is to get the variable by itself.

Example:
(x/3) + 2x - 15 > 13

Add 15 to both sides:
(x/3) + 2x > 28

Multiply both sdies by 3:
x + 6x > 28
7x > 28

Divide both sides by 7:
x > 4 Final answer: x > 4

Solving Inequalities Using Multiplication or Division

When multiplying or dividing by a positive number, the inequality sign stays the same.

Example:
3x < 12

Divide both sides by 3:
x < 4

Final answer: x < 4

Important Rule: Negative Numbers

If you multiply or divide by a negative number, you must flip the inequality sign.
Example:
-2x > 8

Divide both sides by -2:
x < -4

The sign flipped from > to <.

Solving Multi-Step Inequalities

Multi-step inequalities use the same process as regular equations.

Use PEMDAS in reverse:

  1. Add or subtract first.
  2. Multiply or divide second.
  3. Flip the inequality sign if you multiply or divide by a negative.
Example:
2x - 9
-4
> 12

Multiply both sides by -4 (FLIP THE SIGN):
-8x + 36 < 12

Subtract 36 from both sides:
-8x < -24

Divide both sides by -8 (FLIP THE SIGN):
x > 3

Final answer: x > 3

Using the Distributive Property

Sometimes you need to distribute before solving.

Example:
5(x - 3) < -8x + 10

Distribute the 5:
5x - 15 < -8x + 10

Add 8x to both sides:
13x - 15 < 10

Add 15 to both sides:
13x < 25

Divide both sides by 13:
x < 25 / 13

Final answer: x < 25/13

Compound Inequalities

A compound inequality is two inequalities combined together.

There are two main types:

  • And compound inequalities
  • Or compound inequalities

“And” Compound Inequalities

An and compound inequality means the solution must make both inequalities true.

Example:
2 < x < 8

This means x is greater than 2 and less than 8.

x can be numbers like 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, or decimals like 2.5, 4.25, and 7.99.

x cannot equal 2 or 8 because the symbols are not ≤ or ≥.
Where the two colors intersect is where all the true values of x exist.

Solving an “And” Compound Inequality

Example:
-34 < -4x + 10 < 24

Subtract 10 from all three parts:
-44 < -4x < 14

Divide all three parts by -4.
Since we are dividing by a negative, flip both inequality signs:

11 > x > -14/4

Simplify:
11 > x > -7/2

Rewrite it in normal order:
-7/2 < x < 11

Final answer: -7/2 < x < 11

Solving an “And” Inequality (Written Differently)

Occasionally, an "And" inequality will be written like this

Example:
-6x + 1 > -5 and 3x + 4 ≥ 1

Solve the first inequality:
-6x + 1 > -5
-6x > -6
x < 1

Solve the second inequality:
3x + 4 ≥ 1
3x ≥ -3
x ≥ -1

Put them together:
-1 ≤ x < 1

Final answer: -1 ≤ x < 1

“Or” Compound Inequalities

An or compound inequality means either inequality can be true.

Example:
x ≤ 0 or x > 10

This means x can be less than or equal 0 or greater than 10.

The solution has a gap between 0 and 10.
The gap in between the two colors is where all the true values of x exist. Including the solid line!

Solving an “Or” Compound Inequality

Example:
-2x - 3 ≥ 3 or 3x - 4 > -1

Solve the first inequality:
-2x - 3 ≥ 3
-2x ≥ 6
x ≤ -3

Solve the second inequality:
3x - 4 > -1
3x > 3
x > 1

Put them together:
x ≤ -3 or x > 1

Final answer: x ≤ -3 or x > 1

Simple Review

  • Inequalities compare values using <, >, ≤, and ≥.
  • A dotted line means < or >.
  • A solid line means ≤ or ≥.
  • Shade above for y > or y ≥.
  • Shade below for y < or y ≤.
  • Solve inequalities like equations.
  • Flip the sign when multiplying or dividing by a negative number.
  • Compound inequalities can use and or or.
  • And means the answer must satisfy both inequalities.
  • Or means the answer can satisfy either inequality.
